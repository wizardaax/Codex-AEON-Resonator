## PART THREE: THE VOYNICH DISCOVERY

### The Hypothesis

The Voynich Manuscript (Beinecke MS 408) is carbon dated to 1404-1438 CE. Dawit I ruled 1382-1413. The vellum window overlaps directly with his reign.

The botanical sections show 113 or more stylised plants, approximately 90% unmatched to any European, Middle Eastern, or Asian herbal tradition. Six hundred years of cryptographic and botanical analysis, including NSA cryptographers, AI systems, and every major linguistic framework, has failed to identify them or decode the script.

The initial hypothesis: if the reference flora was Ethiopian highland vegetation from the early 1400s, plants that are now extinct or drastically reduced due to centuries of deforestation, you would not find them in any surviving botanical record. They would look like nothing because nothing like them survived to be documented. The absence of a match is not evidence of invention. It is evidence of a lost reference set.

Supporting the linguistic angle independently: when computational linguists ran statistical analysis comparing Voynichese to known languages, the four closest matches were Hebrew, Arabic, Malay in Arabic script, and Amharic, the language of Ethiopia. This result emerged from independent statistical methods with no connection to the botanical hypothesis.

The contact event is documented: four Ethiopian monks attended the Council of Florence in 1438 to 1445, right at the tail end of the vellum window. Ethiopian delegates including the monk Nicodemus were physically in Italy engaging Italian scholars and exchanging texts. This is recorded in Vatican archives. The radiocarbon window means the vellum was prepared either just before or during that delegation period.

The manuscript enters European collections, reaches Rudolf II circa 1600, then a Jesuit library near Rome, then Yale University in 1969 where it currently resides as Beinecke MS 408.

### The Pipeline

To move from hypothesis to evidence, a quantitative morphological comparison pipeline was constructed. Ethiopian plant metrics were compiled from ethnobotanical databases across three dimensions: stem height in metres, leaf length-to-width ratio, and inflorescence geometry score on a scale of one to four (cylindrical, spike, globular, bell).

Voynich folio metrics were estimated from manuscript descriptions and published analyses using the same three dimensions.

First run used raw Euclidean distance. Key results: f13r to Echinops kebericho: 0.75. f4v to Rosa abyssinica: 1.12. f33v to Lobelia rhynchopetalum: 1.80. f93r to Ensete ventricosum: 2.45. Kniphofia foliosa was identified as a methodological outlier because its leaf ratio of 50.0 dominated distances, requiring normalisation.

Second run applied z-score normalisation across all metrics before computing distances. Four additional Ethiopian endemic species were added: Hagenia abyssinica, Erica arborea, Thymus schimperi, Vernonia amygdalina. Five additional Voynich folios were added: f9v, f2v, f56r, f1v, f7r.
